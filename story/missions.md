# Mission Design

Work-in-progress mission designs. Missions are **house-exclusive** — each house gets its own set, though puzzle types are reused across houses with different answers and narrative framing.

**Important context:**
- The houses did NOT expect to find each other here — each hoped to be the first to the temple
- Mission cards may mention the other houses' names and where they are situated in the temple (flavor text)
- The emotional setting between houses: reluctant cooperation between rivals / competing teams forced together by circumstance
- **The patrons (Lara Croft, Indiana Jones) are NOT present in the temple.** Only representatives of each house are there. The patrons are off in their separate locations.

---

## Act 1 Missions

### Pattern: Crew Missions as Character-Defining Dilemmas

Each house's Crew mission is a **dilemma about a signature tool or item** that the rising floodwater threatens to submerge. The house must decide whether to commit resources (time, scans, trades) to rescue it — or let it go and focus elsewhere. The item is tied to the house's identity and may matter again in later acts.

This pattern reinforces house identity, creates meaningful tradeoffs, and builds character investment in a way that costs nothing narratively if the item is lost (the act moves on).

---

### Drake Delegation — Act 1

#### Crew Mission: The Epoxy Decision

Drake's precision explosives are useless without the **fuse charges** that make them ignite. The fuse charges are at risk of being submerged by the rising floodwater, which would render them — and by extension, all their precision explosives — inert.

**The dilemma:** Explosives inside an ancient temple are genuinely dangerous. Using them could bring the temple down on everyone. But Drake Delegation is defined by their willingness to use dynamite, and leaving the fuse charges behind means losing that capability entirely for the rest of the expedition.

**Consequence if saved:** Drake carries active explosive capability into Act 2 (potential tool for clearing sealed passages, but also a risk).

**Consequence if lost:** Drake enters Act 2 without their signature capability. A desperate, mercenary crew without their tools.

### Jones Junket — Act 1

#### Crew Mission: Retrieve the Ceremonial Whips

Jones Junket has somehow left their **precious ceremonial whips** behind, and the floodwater is slowly submerging them. They must decide whether to retrieve them — and if so, construct a tool to pull them from the rising water.

**The dilemma:** The whips are ceremonial, not strictly necessary — but they are deeply tied to the house's identity (their connection to Indiana Jones's legacy). A Jones expedition without whips is an embarrassment; to some, a spiritual failure.

**Consequence if saved:** Whips remain as a house identity item into Act 2.

**Consequence if lost:** The house is stripped of its ceremonial symbol. Morale impact; possibly affects later narrative flavor.

### Croft Company — Act 1

#### Crew Mission: Retrieve the Grappling Gear

Croft's signature tool — a specialized **grappling hook rig with high-tension cable**. Lara Croft's team wouldn't be Lara's team without it. The rig is on a ledge partially submerged in floodwater.

**The dilemma:** The gear is heavy — really heavy. Carrying it through tight, sealing passages in Act 2 will slow the team down. And the retrieval itself pulls the team away from other missions. But Croft dug her way in from below, which means the team will need to **climb out through the top** in Act 2 as the temple seals. The gear could be the difference between reaching a high ledge and not — but the weight could also cost them precious time in a sealing temple.

**Consequence if saved:** Grappling gear available for Act 2. Specifically tee'd up for a future Act 2 mission: *something glinting in a high corner of an upper chamber, accessible only via grappling hook.* A payoff that rewards the team for making the save.

**Consequence if lost:** Physical ascent in Act 2 becomes much harder. Any climbing-based Act 2 mission is blocked or downgraded.

## Act 1 Flood Missions

One per house. Each house approaches the flood problem through the lens of their identity — Drake forces, Jones analyzes, Croft excavates. The flood cannot be fully stopped (the 20-minute timer is fixed), but successful missions may buy narrative time, protect other missions from damage, or prevent losses.

### Drake — The Stone Wheel

**Narrative setup:** All houses initially believe Drake's dynamite blast caused the flooding. Drake's team finds a heavy stone wheel embedded in the wall. Channels are carved into the floor below it, radiating outward — it's immediately evident to Drake's tactical eye that this thing controls the water flow. But it won't budge. The wheel is completely immovable by brute force alone.

**The puzzle:** Solving the mission is what makes the wheel turn. The team must decode glyph markings, find the correct unlocking sequence, or gather specific clue cards that reveal how to release the mechanism. Force alone won't do it — this is a puzzle that demands Drake think before they act.

**What it reveals:** When finally turned, the wheel redirects water flow, exposing inscriptions on the floor. The water channels are *original features* — the flood isn't caused by Drake's blast. It's a designed system. The temple was built to flood itself. This exonerates Drake (or at least complicates the blame) and reveals the temple is an active machine, not a passive ruin.

**The wheel can be destroyed** — once unlocked, Drake can choose to smash it, which would permanently disable this part of the water control system (possible consequence implications for Act 2).

**Reinforces house identity:** Drake sees a mechanism and immediately reads its purpose — but can't muscle through it. The frustration of knowing what it does but not being able to move it is peak Drake tension. And the choice to destroy it afterward is peak Drake resolution.

---

### Jones — Read the Ancient Drainage (pitch)

Jones's academic mindset: the QRians were meticulous engineers — this place must have a built-in drainage system. The team recognizes valve controls disguised as decorative inscriptions along the walls. They need to decipher the QRian glyphs to find the correct sequence to activate the ancient drainage.

**The puzzle:** Translate inscriptions to find the activation sequence (Type B, C) and put the steps in the right order (Type G). Or: a large glyph wall with the key sequence hidden among decorative glyphs, found via coordinates from other clues (Type E).

**What they discover:** The drainage partially activates — but it's been deliberately sabotaged. The damage is QRian work: same tools, same construction technique. They built a sophisticated drainage system and then systematically crippled it. The QRians didn't just build a flood trap — they disabled the safety systems to make sure no one could stop it.

**Reinforces house identity:** Scholarly, patient, rewarded by careful analysis. Uses the glyph system directly — players are literally doing what the Jones team does: deciphering. A perfect "you ARE the academics" moment. And the discovery that the drainage was sabotaged is a gut punch — Jones solved the puzzle correctly, but the QRians anticipated that someone would.

---

### Croft — The Stone Jigsaw (pitch)

Croft's physical mindset: plug the leak with their hands. Water is pouring through a crack in the wall that used to be a blocked passage. The stones that originally sealed it are scattered on the floor, partially in the water. The team has to physically fit them back together — but there are extra pieces, edges don't match obvious neighbors, and each has a QRian glyph that indicates its correct position.

**The puzzle:** Match stone fragments based on glyph markings and edge shapes (Type J for finding markers, Type A for arrangement, or a hybrid). Could also work as Type G — putting fragments in the correct stacking order.

**What they discover:** When the stones are assembled correctly, they form what initially looks like a religious mosaic — a scene of worship, figures in prayer. But the glyphs embedded in the image are mathematical formulas. The "prayer" is an equation. The "worship scene" is a construction diagram. What looked like a religious mural was actually a functional engineering document dressed in sacred imagery. Religion and mathematics were indistinguishable to the QRians — and the "temple" was never purely religious.

**Reinforces house identity:** Physical, hands-on, excavation-adjacent. They dug in; now they're un-digging to save themselves. The stones feel like artifacts, which lets them stay Lara-coded even in a defensive mission.

### Success/failure model for flood missions

Flood missions operate on **loss aversion**, not reward. They do NOT mechanically alter the 20-minute Act 1 timer.

**Success:** Nothing bad happens. Act 2 proceeds as it should. The host's act-break narration names exactly what was saved — concrete and legible (e.g., *"Your precision explosives stayed dry. You'll need them."*).

**Failure:** A specific Act 2 mission gets a **warning** consequence attached (via the existing consequence system) — making it harder, not removing it entirely. Warnings sting without amputating content.

Per-house failure consequences (proposed, tune later):

- **Drake fails Controlled Collapse:** Warning on an Act 2 mission — *"The water surge compromised your remaining precision explosives — you'll need to find another way to clear sealed passages."*
- **Jones fails Drainage:** Warning on an Act 2 Temple Investigation mission — *"The inscriptions here were partially washed away — key context is missing."*
- **Croft fails Stone Jigsaw:** Warning on an Act 2 mission — *"The passage you'd planned is impassable; you have to route around, losing time."*

Crew mission failures already work the same way (lost whips, lost fuse charges, lost grappling gear). Act 1 as a whole is thematically "protect what matters before the temple takes it."

## Act 1 Temple Investigation Missions — Framework

Three per house (9 total). These deliver the Act 1 knowledge baseline.

### Mission Brief Formula

Each Temple Investigation mission brief (on the story card) follows this structure, written in **second person**:

**1. Sight of Interest** — Something the team notices in the chamber that demands attention:

| Sight Type | Examples |
|------------|----------|
| **Unreachable artifact** | A glistening vase on a ledge, a large strange-looking astrolabe, a shimmering object half-submerged |
| **Unreachable utility** | A strange visible lever, a sliding construction in the wall, a mechanism with moving parts |
| **Weird path / possibility of escape** | A darkened hallway, a handhold leading to a pockmarked upper area, a gap in the wall with air flowing through |

**2. The Challenge** — What the team must do to reach/access/investigate the sight. This is where the puzzle lives. May require specific clue cards, tools, or collaboration.

**3. Resulting Evidence** — What investigation reveals. Each result is one or more of:

| Evidence Type | Description |
|---------------|-------------|
| **Previous expedition** | What an earlier group uncovered, or what their existence here reveals |
| **QRian suffering** | Evidence of pain, desperation, confinement, loss |
| **QRian technological might** | Impossibly advanced techniques, engineering, materials |
| **QRian culture** | Religion, language, art, daily life, social structure |
| **Self-influence by the Source** | Players themselves realize something is affecting their own conscious experience. **Act 2+ only — not used in Act 1.** |

### Example mission brief (second-person style)

> **Investigate the Impossible Artifact**
>
> Far off to the left, right at the fuzzy edge of the shadows, your team notices, right atop a ledge, a vibrantly red, sparkling porcelain vase. It seems impossibly intact and clean after all these years. But how? What must it mean? You could wander over to the area and find a way to work together to reach the edge of the 15-foot ledge. You'll just need some tools to scale the wall... Upon solving the mission and getting access to the vase, investigation may reveal that not only is the vase perfectly elliptical, it shows the QRians had access to pottery techniques hundreds of years more advanced than their contemporaries... How is that possible?
>
> - **Sight:** Unreachable artifact (porcelain vase on a 15-foot ledge)
> - **Evidence:** QRian technological might

### The 9 Missions

Each house gets one of each sight type (artifact, utility, path).

### Drake Delegation — Temple Investigation

#### Drake T1 — The Impossible Vase *(Unreachable Artifact)*

**Sight:** Far off to the left, right at the fuzzy edge of the shadows — atop a ledge, a vibrantly red, sparkling porcelain vase. It seems impossibly intact and clean after all these years. But how? What must it mean?

**Challenge:** Wander over to the area and find a way to work together to reach the 15-foot ledge. Drake's tactical gear and comfort with brute problem-solving is an asset.

**Evidence:** QRian technological might. The vase is perfectly elliptical, showing the QRians had access to pottery techniques hundreds of years more advanced than their contemporaries. The material itself is strange — harder than any ceramic the team has seen, which explains why it's survived intact. How did a civilization this advanced end up extinct?

**Danger:** Everything about this place suggests a people who were impossibly ahead of their time. What happened to them?

**Puzzle candidates:** H (Glyph Arithmetic) to decode markings on the vase, or J (Hidden Symbol Hunt) to find maker's marks

---

#### Drake T2 — The Construction Hoist *(Unreachable Utility)*

**Sight:** Directly overhead, near the center of the chamber — thick iron chains hang from the dark above, taut and corroded green, anchored to something massive you can't quite see. They drop straight down to a stone slab on the floor, pulled tight against it like whatever's beneath is straining to get out.

**Challenge:** Figure out how to operate the hoist. Drake's comfort with mechanical gear and brute-force problem-solving is the asset — counterweights need adjusting, seized chains need freeing, and the activation sequence needs decoding from glyph markings on the mechanism itself.

**Evidence:** QRian technological might + built quickly. When activated, the hoist lifts the stone panel off the floor, revealing construction markings underneath: shift tallies, build-speed notations, workforce counts. The numbers are staggering — thousands of workers, weeks of effort. This temple was built in months, not years. The hoist itself is evidence too — this is industrial-scale equipment, not religious architecture. Nobody builds a crane inside a temple unless they were building at speed and scale that demanded heavy machinery.

**Danger:** The haste is the tell. What was so urgent that an entire civilization mobilized thousands of workers and heavy equipment to build a temple in weeks?

**Puzzle candidates:** H (Glyph Arithmetic) to decode the construction tallies, or B (Glyph Cipher) to read the activation sequence on the mechanism

---

#### Drake T3 — The Scraped Gap *(Weird Path)*

**Sight:** Along the back wall, half-hidden behind a collapsed pillar — a dark crack in the stone, just wide enough that a person could squeeze through sideways. Both edges are scored with long, bright scratches — fresh-looking against the ancient stone, as if something metal was forced through recently. From inside, a faint breeze that smells like dust and something else.

**Challenge:** Navigate the tight passage. Requires commitment — whoever goes in is out of action for the other missions for a while.

**Evidence:** Previous expedition. On the other side: the remains of a camp. Lanterns, rope, journals — in a modern language. Another expedition was here, years ago. They never came out. The metal scrape marks match the kind of gear strewn around the camp — this is how the previous team pushed their equipment through. Early journal entries are jubilant — they were "so excited" to be the first to discover the temple, and planned to report it once they got out. Later entries describe growing unease. The final note is a chilling observation: *"Every staircase descends. We cannot find a single path that leads up."*

**Danger:** This isn't the first team to enter. The last team is still here.

**Puzzle candidates:** A (Anagram Assembly) to reconstruct damaged journal pages, or G (Sequence Ordering) to piece together the expedition's timeline

### Jones Junket — Temple Investigation

#### Jones T1 — The Elevated Archive *(Unreachable Artifact)*

**Sight:** As your eyes crawl up the right side of the stone chamber — a shaft of light falls from a crack above, illuminating a row of pale stone tablets nestled in carved alcoves, arranged like offerings to the sky. One has shifted, tilting forward at an angle, and the glyphs on its face catch the light: round, flowing shapes, nothing like the rigid carvings on the walls below. They almost look like a different language entirely.

**Challenge:** Reach the elevated alcoves and carefully extract the tablets without damaging them. Jones's patience and meticulousness is built for exactly this.

**Evidence:** QRian culture + language changed. The tablets are early-period records — written in a dramatically different glyph style from the late-period walls. They document rituals that are simultaneously prayers AND mathematical proofs. The same glyph means both "divine" and "proven." Religion and mathematics were the same discipline.

**Danger:** The language didn't just evolve — it was *transformed*. Something reshaped how these people thought, and the reshape shows in their writing.

**Puzzle candidates:** G (Sequence Ordering) to arrange tablets by era, or C (Conceptual Decipherment) to decode the prayer-proofs

---

#### Jones T2 — The Sliding Panels *(Unreachable Utility)*

**Sight:** Peering straight across the haze of the chamber, a section of wall that looks *wrong* — the stone is divided into panels with thin, precise grooves between them, like a puzzle box built into the architecture. One panel has a dark, hand-shaped hollow worn smooth by use. Someone pressed their palm here, many times.

**Challenge:** The wall has spaces for 9 panels, but several have been dislodged and scattered across the chamber floor. Some of the missing panels appear to have been picked up by the other expedition teams. You'll need to trade to get them back. Each panel has a QRian glyph on one side — scanning reveals the translated word. Write each word on an index card, rearrange until the sentence forms. Enter the panel numbers in correct order.

**Puzzle type:** A — Anagram Assembly

**Required clue cards:** 3 cards across different sets — Panel Set 1 (3 panels), Panel Set 2 (3 panels), Panel Set 3 (3 panels). 9 panels total. Other houses have found some of the scattered panels. *(Card set assignment TBD.)*

| Panel | Word | Fragment |
|-------|------|----------|
| 1 | SEALED | 1 |
| 2 | TOO | 1 |
| 3 | FOREVER | 1 |
| 4 | THOSE | 2 |
| 5 | AWAY | 2 |
| 6 | LONG | 2 |
| 7 | WERE | 3 |
| 8 | WHO | 3 |
| 9 | STAYED | 3 |

**Answer:** `489627153` (THOSE WHO STAYED TOO LONG WERE SEALED AWAY FOREVER)

**Correct answer reveal:**
"The panels slide open. Behind them — a small, sealed chamber. The walls are covered in carvings. Not official inscriptions — personal ones. Someone lived in here. The markings are dense, obsessive, but you can't decipher them yet. Whatever happened to this person, the QRians thought sealing them away was the answer. The chamber smells like dust and centuries."

**Evidence:** QRian suffering. People were sealed in this temple. The carvings hint at something deeper but remain undecipherable in Act 1 — a seed planted for Act 2.

**Danger:** The QRians sealed their own people away. Why? What did they do — or what was done to them?

**Success consequence:**
"You opened something the QRians wanted kept shut. The team is rattled — but also electrified. Whatever this temple was built to contain, you're getting closer to understanding it. Dr. Jones never mentioned anything like this in his lectures. You all feel like you are going to be historical legends, right when your careers start."

**Failure consequence:**
"The panels sit there, immovable, mocking. The hand-shaped hollow stares back at you — worn smooth by centuries of palms that knew the answer. Yours wasn't one of them. Dr. Jones would have had this in minutes. The team tries not to say that out loud, but everyone's thinking it. Morale takes a massive hit..."

#### Jones T3 — The False Exit *(Weird Path)*

**Sight:** Something was odd when you first entered the chamber, and now you realize what. Off to the left, beyond a low archway — a corridor that tilts *up*. Subtle — you'd miss it if you weren't paying attention — but the floor rises. After a temple where everything descends, an upward slope feels like sunlight. Jones's team — the ones who spent three months on schematics — would be the first to notice it from across the room.

**Challenge:** Follow the passage and map it. The team must track their route carefully as it winds upward, curves, and curves again. Glyph markings along the walls provide clues — or misdirection.

**Evidence:** Built to trap. The passage deposits the team back exactly where they started, one level *lower*. It's a deliberate architectural deception — designed to give the feeling of escape while pulling you deeper into the temple. The walls are covered in a repeating QRian message. When deciphered: *"You are not leaving. You were never going to leave."*

**Danger:** This trap was designed for people exactly like them — scholars who would notice an upward passage and follow it. The QRians anticipated that the smartest people in the building would be the first to try to escape, and built a maze specifically to deceive them.

**Puzzle candidates:** D (Maze Routing) to trace the correct path and realize the deception, or B (Glyph Cipher) to decode the repeating wall message

### Croft Company — Temple Investigation

#### Croft T1 — The Shadow Astrolabe *(Unreachable Artifact)*

**Sight:** Deep in the far corner, high up — a glint in the dark, like a single star in a black sky. As your eyes focus, you see concentric rings, dark as iron, catching torchlight in flashes — nested inside each other and etched with markings so fine they shimmer. It's by far the most beautiful thing in this entire temple, and it's sitting on a ledge fifteen feet above the floor.

**Challenge:** Physical climb or teamwork to reach a high ledge. Croft's athleticism and excavation instincts are the asset — human pyramids, leverage from debris, boosting each other up.

**Evidence:** QRian technological might + built to trap. The device maps something, but not stars. The geometric patterns correspond to the temple's own internal layout — it's a building map. It shows chambers they haven't found yet. Every corridor, every staircase, every passage terminates *downward*. The paths that look like they lead up curve back down. The spaces that seem like exits are dead ends. The temple was engineered so that anyone who entered could not traditionally escape — it was designed to *feel* like it has exits while making escape impossible. Labels appear in two distinct glyph styles (early fluid, late geometric), showing the language changed even during the building's use.

**Danger:** This isn't just a building with no exit. It's a trap engineered to be inescapable — one that tricks you into thinking you can get out.

**Puzzle type:** A — Anagram Assembly

**Required clue cards:** 3 cards across different sets — Bundle 1 (2 spokes), Bundle 2 (3 spokes), Bundle 3 (4 spokes). 9 metal spokes total. The team already has a hammer.

**How it works:** Each spoke is numbered and has a translated QRian word inscribed on it. The QRians designed the spokes to be driven in a specific order — top to bottom, they read a QRian sentence. Players write each word on an index card and rearrange until the sentence forms. They enter the spoke numbers in correct order.

| Spoke | Word | Bundle |
|-------|------|--------|
| 1 | FIND | 1 |
| 2 | EVER | 1 |
| 3 | WHO | 2 |
| 4 | WAY | 2 |
| 5 | WILL | 2 |
| 6 | THOSE | 3 |
| 7 | NO | 3 |
| 8 | ENTER | 3 |
| 9 | OUT | 3 |

**Answer:** `638517492` (THOSE WHO ENTER WILL FIND NO WAY OUT EVER)

**Success consequence:**
"The climb was clean. The team moved like one body — boost, grip, pull. Lara would have been proud. Confidence is high, and you've already bagged something that should make this whole expedition historically monumental. You all guard it jealously."

**Failure consequence:**
"Fifteen feet. You couldn't make fifteen feet. And now the floodwater has slowly swallowed the priceless, mysterious device forever. Every other team probably saw you try. What would Lara say? Morale takes a serious hit, and you all wonder how you'll fare with the next chambers to come..."

#### Croft T2 — The Teaching Stone *(Unreachable Utility)*

**Sight:** In the dead center of the chamber, a wide stone disc sits on a pedestal — pale, smooth, almost luminous against the dark walls. A perfect circle is carved into its face, and around the rim, small rectangular slots are cut at precise intervals, like teeth on a gear. It looks like it's waiting for something to be inserted. Behind it, an entire wall of glyphs — dense, deliberate, instructional.

**Challenge:** Find or construct the correct inserts for the slots from gathered clue cards, then activate the mechanism.

**Evidence:** QRian culture. The "altar" is a teaching machine. When activated, moving stone pieces mechanically demonstrate a mathematical principle. The QRians didn't just use math — they built physical devices to venerate it. This wasn't a temple of worship. It was a school of obsessive mathematical devotion. The instruction glyphs treat "to calculate" and "to pray" as the same verb.

**Danger:** There's a line between reverence for knowledge and compulsive worship of order. The QRians crossed it — but the players don't yet know why.

**Puzzle candidates:** C (Conceptual Decipherment) for the glyph instructions, or H (Glyph Arithmetic) to operate the teaching mechanism

---

#### Croft T3 — The Drag Marks *(Weird Path)*

**Sight:** Against the near wall, low and easy to miss — a squat doorway set into stone that's rougher, cruder than the rest, as if finished in a hurry. Beyond it, a corridor so dark the torchlight dies a few feet in. On the floor, gouged into the stone: deep parallel grooves, wide as a body, dragging inward into the black.

**Challenge:** Follow the passage. Requires nerve — it gets darker, tighter, and the air changes.

**Evidence:** QRian suffering + built quickly + built to trap. The passage leads to a sealed chamber. Inside: remains of the people who built this section. Tools, personal effects, unfinished carvings. They weren't allowed to leave after construction. The drag marks were them being brought here. One wall has a single, carefully carved personal message — different from all official inscriptions — a plea in early-period glyphs from someone who knew they would die here.

**Danger:** The QRians sealed their own builders inside the walls. The temple was built on the bodies of the people who made it.

**Puzzle candidates:** B (Glyph Cipher) to decode the personal plea, or K (Reverse Decipherment) to reconstruct the early-period message
