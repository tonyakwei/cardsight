# Mission Design Principles

Evergreen principles for designing missions and story card briefs across all acts.

## Mission Structure

1. **Sight → Challenge → Evidence formula.** Every temple investigation mission starts with a Sight of Interest, presents a Challenge to reach/access it, and delivers Resulting Evidence about the QRians.

2. **Three sight types per house.** Each house gets one unreachable artifact, one unreachable utility, and one weird path — ensures physical variety.

3. **Evidence types are distinct from revelations.** Evidence types (previous expedition, QRian suffering, technological might, culture, self-influence by Source) are the *flavor*. Revelations are the *knowledge baseline* players should reach. Multiple evidence types can deliver the same revelation.

4. **Revelation coverage requires redundancy.** Every revelation should be hit by 2+ missions across different houses, so missing one doesn't break the knowledge arc.

5. **Loss aversion, not reward.** Mission success means avoiding a specific loss (consequence in the next act), not earning a bonus. Concrete and legible ("your explosives stayed dry") beats abstract ("you gained some extra time").

6. **Crew missions need balanced dilemmas.** The signature item shouldn't be obviously worth saving — it needs a real downside (weight, danger, opportunity cost) so the choice is genuine.

## Story Card Briefs

7. **Visual-first.** Every sight should be something you could point at across a torchlit room before you understand what it is. Color, light, shape, contrast — not technical descriptions.

8. **Prepositional anchor.** Each sight opens with a spatial phrase placing it relative to the team's standing position: "Far off to the left...", "Directly overhead...", "Against the near wall..."

9. **Standing vantage point.** Sights are described as if the team is standing still and looking, never "walking past" or "almost missing" something in motion.

10. **Tone mix — wonder and grim.** Some sights should be beautiful and alluring, others unsettling. The temple is fascinating AND dangerous.

11. **Suggestive action language.** "You could...", "With enough effort...", "scaling the ledge should be possible..." — never imperatives. Players choose to engage, they're not commanded.

12. **Brevity.** Players use the story card as a game interface, not a story to read. Each mission brief is 3-5 sentences max.

13. **Trading hook.** When a mission requires clue cards from other teams, the brief should end with a house-voiced hint about acquiring them, with trailing ellipsis. Drake "liberates," Jones "negotiates," Croft "acquires." The hint names the clue category so players know what to ask for.

14. **Titles describe what you can do.** "Reach the Impossible Vase", "Investigate the Scraped Gap" — action-oriented, not lore-oriented.

15. **Second person plural throughout.** "Your tactical eyes", not "your tactical eye."

16. **House voice and humor.** Each house's briefs should sound like *that house* wrote them. Drake = dry mercenary pragmatism. Humor is injected into the briefs, not the intro.

17. **House intro notices other teams.** The story card intro names the other houses with brief, character-revealing visual descriptions and locations in the room.

## Mission Consequences

18. **Three distinct texts per mission.** (a) **Correct answer reveal** — shown on phone immediately after solving; the "what you found" moment, narrative payoff. (b) **Success consequence** — read at act break; about how succeeding affected the team's morale, dynamics, confidence. (c) **Failure consequence** — read at act break; about how failing affected the team. Keep revelations appropriate to the act's knowledge baseline — don't jump ahead.

19. **Consequences are personal, not informational.** Don't write "you learned X" or "you missed information Y." Write about morale, team dynamics, relationships, what their patron would think. The consequence should affect how the *house feels about itself*.

20. **Success ends with forward momentum.** Pride, confidence, possessiveness, excitement. The team should feel like they've earned something and are ready for more. End with an active emotional beat — "you guard it jealously," "confidence is high."

21. **Failure ends with dread about what's ahead.** Not just "you failed" but "...and the next part is going to be harder because of it." The ellipsis, the trailing worry, the weight of what's coming. End looking forward into the dark, not backward at the mistake.

22. **Name the loss concretely.** If something was lost, say what — "the floodwater swallowed the device forever." Abstract failure ("you didn't succeed") has no sting. Concrete loss ("it's gone, and everyone saw you fail") hurts.

## Experimental: Puzzle QA Checklist

*Added 2026-04-24. Trial run — remove this section if it causes more friction than it prevents.*

Before presenting a puzzle design, run these checks:

- **Player walkthrough.** Step through the puzzle as a player, in order. What do they see first? What information do they have at each step? If the timing doesn't work, the puzzle doesn't work.
- **Adversarial test each answer.** For every answer, ask: "What OTHER answer could a reasonable player give?" If there's a plausible alternative, flag it as ambiguous.
- **Diegetic sentence.** State in one sentence why solving the puzzle enables the physical action. If that sentence doesn't exist, the connection is broken.
- **Simplest setup first.** What's the simplest physical object that delivers this puzzle? Start there, not with the most dramatic one.
- **Don't invent constraints.** If a rule or conflict isn't written down, ask — don't assert.
- **Fact-check physical descriptions.** If naming a real material or object, verify basic properties.
