# Mission Design Principles

Evergreen principles for designing missions and story card briefs across all acts.

## Mission Structure

1. **Sight → Challenge → Evidence formula.** Every temple investigation mission starts with a Sight of Interest, presents a Challenge to reach/access it, and delivers Resulting Evidence about the QRians.

2. **Three sight types per house.** Each house gets one unreachable artifact, one unreachable utility, and one weird path — ensures physical variety.

3. **Evidence types are distinct from revelations.** Evidence types (previous expedition, QRian suffering, technological might, culture, self-influence by Source) are the *flavor*. Revelations are the *knowledge baseline* players should reach. Multiple evidence types can deliver the same revelation.

4. **Revelation coverage requires redundancy.** Every revelation should be hit by 2+ missions across different houses, so missing one doesn't break the knowledge arc.

5. **Loss aversion, not reward.** Mission success means avoiding a specific loss (consequence in the next act), not earning a bonus. Concrete and legible ("your explosives stayed dry") beats abstract ("you gained some extra time").

6. **Crew missions need balanced dilemmas.** The signature item shouldn't be obviously worth saving — it needs a real downside (weight, danger, opportunity cost) so the choice is genuine.

## Story Card Briefs

7. **Visual-first.** Every sight should be something you could point at across a torchlit room before you understand what it is. Color, light, shape, contrast — not technical descriptions.

8. **Prepositional anchor.** Each sight opens with a spatial phrase placing it relative to the team's standing position: "Far off to the left...", "Directly overhead...", "Against the near wall..."

9. **Standing vantage point.** Sights are described as if the team is standing still and looking, never "walking past" or "almost missing" something in motion.

10. **Tone mix — wonder and grim.** Some sights should be beautiful and alluring, others unsettling. The temple is fascinating AND dangerous.

11. **Suggestive action language.** "You could...", "With enough effort...", "scaling the ledge should be possible..." — never imperatives. Players choose to engage, they're not commanded.

12. **Brevity.** Players use the story card as a game interface, not a story to read. Each mission brief is 3-5 sentences max.

13. **Titles describe what you can do.** "Reach the Impossible Vase", "Investigate the Scraped Gap" — action-oriented, not lore-oriented.

14. **Second person plural throughout.** "Your tactical eyes", not "your tactical eye."

15. **House voice and humor.** Each house's briefs should sound like *that house* wrote them. Drake = dry mercenary pragmatism. Humor is injected into the briefs, not the intro.

16. **House intro notices other teams.** The story card intro names the other houses with brief, character-revealing visual descriptions and locations in the room.
